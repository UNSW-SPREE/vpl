Assignment 4 (Phosphorus Diffusion)

Assignment 4 focuses on the formation of the emitter through the diffusion of phosphorus into the surface of the silicon at high temperature.  It is essential the wafers be extremely clean for this high temperature process.  Following the application of the diffusion source to the surface of the wafer by spraying, or spin-on techniques, or some other deposition approach, the diffusion process is carried out in a belt furnace, with the first section of the furnace being the drying zone to ensure the diffusion source is dry.  The processing variables to be user specified for each diffusion process are :

  • Belt speed (which affects both the drying and the diffusion time);
  • Drying temperature;
  • Zone 1, Zone 2 and Zone 3 temperatures;
  • Nitrogen gas flow;
  • Phosphorus source concentration.

Zone 1 and Zone 3 temperatures should be selected to be approximately 200 degrees below the Zone 2 temperature.  At these temperatures Zones 1 & 3 do not make a serious contribution to the diffusion process but are important in minimising the thermal gradients when approaching and departing the Zone 2.  All other processing parameters for all other processes throughout the production line remain fixed at the default values and therefore cannot be varied for this assignment.

The assignment batch contains 10 wafers. You may process as many batches as you like while trying to optimise the processing parameters of the “phosphorus diffusion“.  Your overall aim is to achieve the highest performance cells but while simultaneously achieving good yields.  There are help files that can be accessed to help you understand and learn about the significance of these diffusion parameters and their optimisation.  There are also various tests you can perform on finished and partially finished cells to help monitor performance during processing. You may process multiple “Assignment batches“, in order to achieve the best possible results.  You will submit the results from one batch to satisfy Task A and may submit the results from separate batches to satisfy the requirements of the other Tasks.   Often in manufacturing, we find that the batch that produces the highest efficiency cell (as required for task B), does not necessarily produce the highest average cell efficiency for the whole batch (as required for task C).  Each task is worth 25% of the marks, with the efficiencies/lifetimes achieved directly determining the marks awarded:

Assessable Tasks (all tasks are of equal value)

Task A:    To produce the cell with the highest pseudo efficiency (which can be measured directly following the diffusion process)

Task B:    To produce an Assignment batch with the highest efficiency single cell. The quality of the remainder of the cells does not matter.  (note: The higher the efficiency the higher the mark)

Task C:    To produce an Assignment batch with the highest average cell efficiency over the 10 cells. (Note that efficiency of broken cells is 0).

Task D:    To produce the wafer at the end of the diffusion process with the highest possible minority carrier lifetime.

Study Questions

1. Why are the pseudo efficiencies so much higher than the real cell efficiencies?

2. Why does optimising the pseudo efficiencies often cause the real cell efficiencies to fall?

3. Higher minority carrier lifetimes will in general improve the cell performance.  Why does this not always seem to happen when we are phosphorus diffusing the cells?

4.  How are the sheet resistivity measurements able to help you when optimising these processes?

5. Can you explain the differences in the spectral responses for the cells satisfying tasks A & B?

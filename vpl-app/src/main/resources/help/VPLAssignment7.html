Assignment 7 (Silver Screen-printing and Firing)

Assignment 7 focuses on the formation of the front metal grid using the screen-printing of silver paste followed by high temperature firing to facilitate ohmic contact between the ag and the Si.  The process can be split into two, a printing step followed by the drying and firing process in the furnace.   For the printing process, the user controllable parameters include the strand diameter of the mesh for the screen, the number of strands/cm for the screen mesh, the emulsion thicknesses for the top and bottom surfaces of the screen that define the pattern,  the finger spacing, the paste viscosity and the squeegee pressure and speed.  The drying/firing process is carried out in a belt furnace, with the first section of the furnace being the drying zone to ensure the paste is dry.  The processing variables to be user specified for the firing process are : belt speed (which affects both the drying and the metal firing time); drying temperature; and the Zone 1, Zone 2 and Zone 3 temperatures. Zone 1 and Zone 3 temperatures should be selected to be approximately 150 degrees below the Zone 2 temperature so as to help maintain a uniform temperature profile across the Zone 2. These two zones should also be significantly shorter in length than the Zone 2.   All other processing parameters for all other processes throughout the production line remain fixed at the default values and therefore cannot be varied for this assignment.

The assignment batch contains 10 wafers. You may process as many batches as you like while trying to optimise the processing parameters of the “silver screen printing and firing“.  Your overall aim is to achieve the highest performance cells but while simultaneously achieving good yields.  There are help files that can be accessed to help you understand and learn about the significance of these aluminium screen-printing and firing parameters and their optimisation.  There are a large range of tests you can perform on the finished cells to help diagnose any weaknesses. The primary aim in optimising the processing conditions is to produce a final IV curve that as closely as possible approximates the pseudo IV curve from prior to the silver contact application.  This involves minimising the series resistive losses (Rs) associated with extracting the generated current from the solar cell but while simultaneously trying to minimise shading losses. The shading losses can be observed through the reflection test while insight into the Rs losses can be gained through the IV curve, the pseudo IV curve, the series resistance, the silver paste thickness (before and after firing) and the silver resistance test. You may process multiple “Assignment batches“, in order to achieve the best possible results.  You will submit the results from one batch to satisfy Task A and may submit the results from separate batches to satisfy the requirements of the other Tasks.   Each task is worth 25% of the marks, with the efficiencies achieved directly determining the marks awarded:

Assessable Tasks (all tasks are of equal value)

Task A:    To produce the wafer at the end of the printing process (and before firing) that has the thickest layer of aluminium paste.

Task B:    To produce an Assignment batch with the highest average cell efficiency over the 10 cells.

Task C:    To produce the lowest possible series resistance for a single cell.

Task D:    To produce the cell with the highest pseudo efficiency at the end of processing.

Study Questions

3. Why can the inclusion of an AR coating help both the series resistance and also perhaps the cell short wavelength response?

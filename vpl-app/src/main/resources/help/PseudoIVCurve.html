Pseudo Current-Voltage (IV) Measurements

True current-voltage measurements can only be made once both metal contacts have been applied to the solar cell surfaces.  It is very useful, however, to be able to measure the pseudo I-V curve prior to applying the metal contacts.  This test is quite similar in many ways to that used for determining the minority carrier lifetimes for the material.

The pseudo IV measurements rely on pulsing the wafer with a bright light and taking rapid measurements of the wafer’s photoconductivity as a function of the light intensity as the light pulse decays.  As explained in the section relating to lifetime measurement tests, the photoconductance measurement relates directly to the minority carrier concentrations in the wafer at the particular light intensity.  This in turn can be equated to a voltage that would be produced by that wafer at that carrier concentration.  It is therefore possible to determine an implied voltage for each light intensity, with each light intensity in turn, being equivalent to a certain current flowing through the device.  An implied I-V relationship can therefore be determined which we describe as being a pseudo-I-V curve.

The pseudo I-V curve differs from the real I-V curve that would be produced by an equivalent device, as there are no resistive losses within the semiconductor material or at the metal/silicon interfaces or within the metal itself.  It therefore produces an IV curve that is the same shape that would be expected from the finished device if the latter had its series resistance losses removed.  This test will therefore provide useful feedback on other loss mechanisms within the device other than those related to series resistance losses.  These include shunting effects or losses in fill-factor resulting from junction recombination (observed through a high ideality factor).  The pseudo IV curve will also give a good indication as to the likely open circuit for the completed device.

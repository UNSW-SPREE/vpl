ALUMINIUM FIRING

Level 1 - Aluminium Firing

Following screen-printing, the aluminium paste is dried so as to remove the majority of the solvents and thinners.  Following drying, the belt travels through to a high temperature zone typically in the 800-900o temperature range.  At these temperatures, the aluminium powder within the paste melts, therefore forming a layer of aluminium at the rear.  Also at these temperatures, the aluminium is able to alloy to the silicon with the aluminium/silicon eutectic temperature being only 577oC.


The length of the firing process is determined by the belt speed (belt furnace shown below).  It needs to be long enough to enable some alloying to take place but not so long as to result in all the aluminium from the rear being consumed by the silicon.  Aluminium in silicon is able to act as a p-type dopant as a result of it being a valence 3 element.

At these temperatures, the solid solubility of the aluminium within the silicon is approximately 1019 atoms/cm3.  This level of p-type doping is well above that used in normal p-type silicon wafers, therefore providing a back surface field across the rear surface.  The heavy p-type doping within this region acts to reduce the minority carrier concentration (electrons) therefore reducing the number of electrons available close to the metal/silicon interface at the rear surface for recombining.  Doping underneath metal contacts is therefore beneficial for reducing recombination at the metal/silicon interface. 


The p-type doping gradient towards the rear also has a small electric field associated with it in such a direction as to repel the minority carriers (electrons) away from the rear surface, therefore also acting to reduce rear surface recombination.  The nett result of reducing the recombination towards the rear is that devices achieve higher open circuit voltages (through the reduction in dark saturation current) while simultaneously achieving higher collection probabilities for carriers generated in the rear region of the device which therefore leads to an increased short circuit current.  In particular, infrared light which is absorbed particularly weakly in silicon, is often responsible for generating electron-hole pairs in the vicinity of the rear metal contact.  These electron-hole pairs can only contribute to the short circuit current provided the recombination at the rear surface can be kept sufficiently low, an achievement aided by the presence of the aluminium alloyed back surface field.

Level 2 - Aluminium Firing

Firing of the rear metal contact is one of the more critical processes in fabricating screen-printed solar cells.  This is because the front surfaces of the wafers are facing downwards and are in contact with the screen printer base plate, the conveyor belts and eventually the infrared furnace metal belt.

Excessive vibration or pressure or contamination of these transport mechanisms will lead to damage to the front surface of the wafer, particularly if the wafer is textured (as shown).  This will lead to corresponding degraded electrical performance due to probable shunting of the junction which is located near the front surface.  Shunting will usually eventuate following the high temperature process during which damage at the front surface or contamination from metals will penetrate through to the junction region and activate recombination or shunting processes. 


Of perhaps even greater importance is the necessity to keep the screen printer base plate, the rear of the screen itself, the conveyor belts and the infrared furnace belt, all free from contamination by the aluminium paste.  Common contributors to this problem are broken wafers, messy operators or even excessively runny aluminium paste.  Regular inspections of all four areas are essential as any aluminium contamination in these regions will be transferred to the front surface of wafers leading to the aluminium being alloyed into the emitter and through the junction, inevitably leading to severe shunting. 


At the end of the firing process, a simple test can be used to identify any aluminium contamination of the front surface.  This test is called Voc contour test which involves placing the wafer under a light source as shown below and then using a multimeter to probe the voltage formed between the rear aluminium metal and the n-type diffused front surface.  The voltages measured should be all uniform except for a slight degradation towards the edges if edge junction isolation has not been carried out effectively. 


Any aluminium contamination will lead to a severe fall in voltage measured in the immediate vicinity with a fairly steep gradient in the voltage laterally across the wafer surface as the distance away from the contamination increases as shown below. 


It is important to properly dry the aluminium paste prior to the wafers reaching the high temperature firing zone.  Any solvents or thinners remaining when the high temperature zone is reached will lead to their explosive evolution from within the paste with the resulting tendency being to form a damaged and much thicker than desirable oxide layer with a greater degree of ferocity.  On the other hand, a certain thickness of rear aluminium oxide layer is required to keep the molten aluminium and molten aluminium/silicon regions in place.  For this purpose, oxygen is required in the ambient.  Inadequate oxidation of the aluminium surface can lead to pimples forming on the rear where the molten aluminium and silicon material penetrates through the aluminium oxide layer.  Such pimples on the rear are quite destructive and will often lead to wafers breaking during subsequent processing, particularly when the wafers are placed on a wafer hold-down such as the base plate for the silver screen printer.


In terms of achieving the best electrical properties for the aluminium back surface field, higher temperatures tend to be preferable as they provide higher solid solubilities and therefore higher doping concentrations for the aluminium within the silicon.  The trade-off, however, is that higher temperatures will lead to increased consumption of the aluminium as more of it is consumed in the alloying process.


In general, therefore, higher temperatures need to be accompanied by higher belt speeds so as to quickly ramp the wafers up to the desired temperature and then subsequently rapidly cool the wafers down to minimise the depth of the alloying process.  For this reason, temperatures are rarely taken much above 900oC as the required thicknesses for the aluminium paste become too great to be economically viable and too thick to reliably print through the screen-printing process.  Even with temperatures in the mid-800’s range, the aluminium paste thickness still, in general, needs to be at least 20 microns thick to facilitate retention of aluminium at the rear for contacting purposes. 


Many manufacturers will choose to screen print narrow strips of silver onto the rear specifically for contacting purposes (as shown).  These contain small quantities of aluminium so as they can be printed into gaps in the pattern for the screen printed aluminium and still make good ohmic contact to the p-type silicon substrate.  Several percent of aluminium can be tolerated within the silver paste without damaging its solderability and this is sufficient to facilitate good ohmic contact formation (but without the high quality back surface field produced by the aluminium screen printed regions). 


There is also evidence that the fast ramp up rate and fast cool down rate for the aluminium alloying process is beneficial for improving the back surface field, independently of the other benefits associated with reducing the consumption of aluminium.  The reasons for the improved electrical properties for the fast ramp rates are not well understood although one theory is that it enhances gettering processes through the increased stress that results on the wafers (gettering refers to the immobilising or extraction of contaminants or defects from within the silicon that would otherwise contribute to recombination processes). 


Another possible explanation for the improved back surface field performance is that the faster cooling rate helps quickly freeze the molten aluminium/silicon region without allowing time for the redistribution of the dopants (aluminium).  The molten alloyed region containing silicon and aluminium forms initially at the interface between the silicon and the aluminium once the temperature exceeds 577oC.  As the temperature continues to rise, more silicon is consumed into the molten region while on the other side of the melt, all the aluminium becomes molten.  While at high temperature, the silicon continues to be dissolved by the molten region with a resultant concentration gradient for the aluminium ranging from almost zero at one edge of the molten region ranging up to 100% at the other edge.  Rapid cooling and hence rapid freezing of this molten region apparently helps retain the described concentration gradient in the aluminium, which appears to be beneficial for the back surface field performance.  Slow cooling inevitably leads to more mixing between the aluminium and silicon and a less optimal concentration gradient for the aluminium within the silicon. 

The described cooling process is often aided by a relatively high belt speed, (compared to other infrared belt furnace processes), and the inclusion of several cooling fans mounted at the tail end of the furnace to blow cool air onto the wafers as soon as they depart from the hot zone.

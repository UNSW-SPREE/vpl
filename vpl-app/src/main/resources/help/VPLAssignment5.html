Assignment 5 (Aluminium Screen-printing and Firing)

Assignment 5 focuses on the formation of the rear metal contact using screen-printed aluminium paste followed by high temperature firing to alloy the Al to the Si.  The process can be split into two, a printing step followed by the drying and firing process in the furnace.  For the printing process, the user controllable parameters include the strand diameter of the mesh for the screen, the number of strands/cm for the screen mesh, the emulsion thicknesses for the top and bottom surfaces of the screen that define the pattern,  the paste viscosity and the squeegee pressure and speed.  The alloying process is carried out in a belt furnace, with the first section of the furnace being the drying zone to ensure the paste is dry.  The processing variables to be user specified for the alloying process are : belt speed (which affects both the drying and the metal firing times); drying temperature; Zone 1, Zone 2 and Zone 3 temperatures; nitrogen gas flow;  and the percentage of oxygen mixed with the nitrogen. Zone 1 and Zone 3 temperatures should again be selected to be approximately 200 degrees below the Zone 2 temperature so as to help maintain a uniform temperature profile across the Zone 2.  All other processing parameters for all other processes throughout the production line remain fixed at the default values and therefore cannot be varied for this assignment.

The assignment batch contains 10 wafers. You may process as many batches as you like while trying to optimise the processing parameters of the “aluminium screening and firing“.  Your overall aim is to achieve the highest performance cells but while simultaneously achieving good yields.  There are help files that can be accessed to help you understand and learn about the significance of these aluminium screen-printing and firing parameters and their optimisation.  There are also various tests you can perform on finished and partially finished cells to help monitor performance during processing. You may process multiple “Assignment batches“, in order to achieve the best possible results.  You will submit the results from one batch to satisfy Task A and may submit the results from separate batches to satisfy the requirements of the other Tasks.   Often in manufacturing, we find that the batch that produces the highest efficiency cell (as required for task B), does not necessarily produce the highest average cell efficiency for the whole batch (as required for task C).  Each task is worth 20% of the marks, with the efficiencies/lifetimes achieved directly determining the marks awarded:

Assessable Tasks (all tasks are of equal value)

Task A:    To produce the cell with the highest pseudo efficiency (which can be measured directly following the plasma etching process)

Task B:    To produce an Assignment batch with the highest efficiency single cell. The quality of the remainder of the cells does not matter.  (note: The higher the efficiency the higher the mark)

Task C:    To produce an Assignment batch with the highest average cell efficiency over the 10 cells. (Note that efficiency of broken cells is 0).

Task D:    To produce the wafer at the end of the aluminium firing process with the highest possible minority carrier lifetime.

Task E:    To produce the wafer at the end of the printing process (and before firing) that has the thickest layer of aluminium paste.

Study Questions

1. For the cell with the highest pseudo efficiency, why is its real cell efficiency so much lower?

2. What yield problems can occur through this process and what are the causes?

3. Higher minority carrier lifetimes will in general improve the cell performance.  Why does this not always seem to happen with the aluminium firing?

4. How can the Voc contour test help solve some of the yield problems and assist with optimising the firing conditions?

5. Why is the oxygen important in the ambient gas?

6. What factors, when designing and using the screen, are responsible for restricting the Al paste thickness and why?
